Memorandum of Law Regarding 13 V.S.A. § 3002, the Absence of a Record-Based Predicate-Offense Classification, and Alternative Relief by Sealing

The question before the Court is not whether the phrase “predicate offense” has been repeated in later denials, but whether the record and governing statutes actually establish that classification. Under Vermont’s current statutory framework, “qualifying crime” includes all misdemeanor offenses except specified exclusions, and the current definition section no longer contains the former standalone definition of “predicate offense” that existed under prior law (13 V.S.A. § 7601, 2026; 2025 Vt. Acts & Resolves No. 60, § 1). The Court’s present analysis must therefore be governed by the text now in force, rather than by continuing to treat a removed statutory definition as if it still controls current classification (13 V.S.A. §§ 7601–7602, 2026). That matters here because 13 V.S.A. § 3002 is not excluded from qualifying-misdemeanor status by the current statutory text (13 V.S.A. § 7601, 2026).

This filing should not be confused with a motion. It is a memorandum of law directed to the facts of the legislation as originally written in the past, as enacted in the present, and as properly applied going forward unless and until the Legislature expressly provides otherwise. The issue is therefore one of statutory meaning and record accuracy, not one of mere repetition of prior labels. The controlling question remains whether Vermont law and the contemporaneous case record actually establish 13 V.S.A. § 3002 as a predicate offense, and on this record they do not (13 V.S.A. §§ 7601–7602, 2026).

The contemporaneous record is direct, and it helps precisely because it fixes the original character of the offense on the books. The February 11, 2014 entry states: “Reparative Board Referral ... 1. IMPERSONATION OF PUBLIC OFFICER ... 08/17/2013 (MIS) 13 VSA 3002 (13V3002),” which records the matter as a misdemeanor offense under § 3002 and nothing more (Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2026). That entry supports the position that Petitioner pled guilty to a nonviolent misdemeanor charge that the court itself recorded as “(MIS) 13 VSA 3002,” not as a listed crime and not as an expressly designated predicate offense (Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2026). If the original legal character of the case had truly been a predicate offense for Chapter 230 purposes, the Court’s own contemporaneous record should reflect that classification, yet the dispositive entry reflects only a misdemeanor referral to the Reparative Board (Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2026).

The broader register and docket history reinforces the same point. The case is identified as a misdemeanor matter, the offense is listed as “IMPERSONATION OF PUBLIC OFFICER,” and the record reflects a guilty plea and disposition under 13 V.S.A. § 3002 without any contemporaneous notation that the conviction was entered as a predicate offense or as some separately excluded category (Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2020, 2026). The later predicate-offense theory therefore appears as a subsequent interpretive label applied in later rulings rather than as an original record-based classification established at plea or disposition (Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2020, 2026). No evidence is therefore provided in the contemporaneous record establishing, as a fact of law, that § 3002 was classified as a predicate offense before the 2025 statutory revision, and repeated later use of that phrase does not supply what the original record does not show (Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2020, 2026).

Petitioner has not been confused about what a predicate offense is, and the record should not suggest otherwise. As Petitioner has previously maintained in earlier filings, the relevant problem is not a failure to understand the term, but the improper extension of that term to 13 V.S.A. § 3002 when the statute is not enumerated in the legislation as a predicate offense and the contemporaneous case record does not classify it as such (Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2026; 13 V.S.A. § 7601, 2026). The older theory effectively assumes that because § 3002 enhances punishment for a second or subsequent offense, it must automatically qualify as a predicate offense for Chapter 230 purposes, but that conclusion is not stated in § 3002 itself and is not part of the current operative definition in § 7601 (13 V.S.A. §§ 3002, 7601, 2026). The legislation and current statutory framing therefore do not support treating § 3002 as a predicate offense merely because it contains an internal repeat-offense enhancement provision (13 V.S.A. §§ 3002, 7601, 2026).

That conclusion is strengthened by the text of the related exclusion provisions. Vermont’s listed-crime definition in 13 V.S.A. § 5301(7) is an enumerated statutory list, and impersonation of officer under § 3002 does not appear in that list (13 V.S.A. § 5301(7), 2026). Likewise, current § 7601 defines qualifying crimes broadly to include misdemeanor offenses except the exclusions specifically identified by statute, and § 3002 is not named among those exclusions in the current law (13 V.S.A. § 7601, 2026). The better reading, then, is that § 3002 remains a misdemeanor offense not excluded from qualifying status under the present statutory framework, even if earlier rulings attempted to place it within a broader and now-removed predicate-offense concept (13 V.S.A. §§ 5301(7), 7601, 2026).

The State’s opposition, as reflected in the current register, does not cure that statutory problem. The hearing minutes from February 26, 2026 reflect only that Defendant requested expungement and that the State opposed, and the minute entry does not set out any developed statutory analysis explaining why § 3002 should continue to be treated as a predicate offense under the current law (Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2026). Petitioner has consistently maintained that the State’s position amounted to unsupported assertion rather than legal analysis, and that is why Petitioner previously stated that the State was “speculating on all accounts” when attempting to preserve the predicate-offense label without adequate legislative or statutory grounding. On this record, Petitioner cannot agree that “because it is” is a legal argument, because the legislation, the statute, and the contemporaneous case entries do not establish the classification the State seeks to preserve (Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2026; 13 V.S.A. §§ 3002, 5301(7), 7601, 2026).

The argument still stands in support of the legislation itself. This matter is a misdemeanor because the law and record establish it as a misdemeanor, not because the State prefers a later label that is unsupported by the original entry. The predicate-offense argument by the State can no longer be used as a sufficient justification to deny all relief where the statutory framing does not establish § 3002 as an enumerated predicate offense. Until the Legislature clearly says otherwise, this matter remains what the law and the court record say it is, namely a misdemeanor under 13 V.S.A. § 3002.

At the same time, this memorandum does not collapse expungement and sealing into the same remedy. Current 13 V.S.A. § 7602 limits expungement of a conviction to circumstances in which the underlying conduct is no longer prohibited by law or designated as a criminal offense, and § 3002 remains an active criminal statute, which is why expungement may fail under the present text (13 V.S.A. §§ 3002, 7602, 2026). But § 7602 separately authorizes sealing for convictions involving qualifying crimes, and § 7607 provides that, upon entry of a sealing order, the person whose record is sealed shall be treated in all respects as if the person had never been arrested, convicted, or sentenced for the offense, while public inspection is tightly restricted (13 V.S.A. §§ 7602, 7607, 2026). That statutory distinction is critical here because the Court may deny expungement under current § 7602(a)(1) and still order sealing as the legally sound alternative that keeps the matter from public view to the fullest extent permitted by law (13 V.S.A. §§ 7602, 7607, 2026).

Conclusion

For the foregoing reasons, the continued reliance on a predicate-offense classification for 13 V.S.A. § 3002 should not control the present analysis because the contemporaneous record does not establish that classification and the current statutory framework no longer turns on that removed definition (13 V.S.A. §§ 7601–7602, 2026; Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2020, 2026). The record instead shows that Petitioner pled guilty to a nonviolent misdemeanor charge recorded as “02/11/2014 Reparative Board Referral … IMPERSONATION OF PUBLIC OFFICER … 08/17/2013 (MIS) 13 VSA 3002 (13V3002),” which materially supports the conclusion that the offense was entered and disposed of as a misdemeanor under § 3002 rather than as an originally designated predicate offense (Vermont Superior Court, Bennington Unit, 2026). This is not a motion, but a memorandum of law pointing to the facts of the legislation as originally written in the past, as enacted in the present, and as properly applied going forward unless and until the Legislature expressly provides otherwise. The argument therefore remains grounded in the legislation itself: on this record and under the current statutory framework, this matter is a misdemeanor under 13 V.S.A. § 3002, not a predicate offense because the State prefers to call it one.

If the Court does not wish to order expungement under current 13 V.S.A. § 7602(a)(1), then the legally sound and equitable remedy is to order sealing. Section 3002 is not a listed crime, is not excluded from qualifying status under the current statute, and sealing would keep this matter from continued public exposure to the fullest extent permitted by law (13 V.S.A. §§ 5301(7), 7601–7602, 7607, 2026). My professional advice is to seal the record, because that remedy aligns with the present statutory framework while recognizing the contemporaneous record for what it actually shows. Petitioner therefore respectfully requests that the Court reject continued use of an unsupported predicate-offense label as the controlling basis for relief and, at minimum, order the record sealed from public view under the governing statutory framework (13 V.S.A. §§ 7601–7602, 7607, 2026).
