# Digital Freedom Letter to Congress

## An Open Letter to the United States House of Representatives and the United States Senate

Dear Members of the United States House of Representatives and the United States Senate,

I write to demand serious federal action to protect digital freedom in the United States. Americans are living under an expanding regime of surveillance, data extraction, algorithmic control, and platform domination that now affects nearly every part of daily life. What was once framed as convenience has become a system in which privacy is weakened, consent is manipulated, reputation is vulnerable to automation, and ordinary people are forced to defend themselves against institutions with vastly greater technical and legal power.

Congress has delayed too long. The United States still lacks a strong, coherent, rights-based federal framework that protects the public from abusive data practices, opaque automated decision-making, exploitative data brokerage, and platform systems capable of restricting visibility, speech, opportunity, and public identity without meaningful due process. This failure is no longer theoretical. It is structural, national, and urgent.

Digital freedom must be treated as a civil and democratic issue, not merely a consumer preference issue. In modern America, access to education, employment, healthcare systems, housing markets, banking, insurance, communication, and civic participation is inseparable from digital systems. When those systems are unaccountable, rights become conditional. When those systems are opaque, power becomes unchallengeable. When those systems are allowed to profile, rank, suppress, or expose individuals without meaningful safeguards, freedom itself is diminished.

The people of this country need enforceable protections, not symbolic language. Congress should enact federal legislation that establishes a clear digital bill of rights for the American public. At minimum, such legislation should guarantee the right to know what personal data is collected, the right to access it, the right to correct false records, the right to demand deletion where retention is not legally required, and the right to portability across systems and platforms. Americans should not be trapped in extractive ecosystems simply because the law failed to keep pace with technological power.

Congress must also directly confront the data broker industry. It is unacceptable that personal information can be collected, packaged, inferred, sold, recirculated, and weaponized by entities the public never knowingly dealt with in the first place. Any serious federal law should impose mandatory registration, strict transparency requirements, broad opt-out and deletion rights, aggressive enforcement penalties, and meaningful remedies when harm results from misuse, inaccuracy, exposure, or unauthorized sale of personal data.

In addition, Congress must regulate high-impact algorithmic systems with far greater seriousness. When automated systems influence credit, employment, housing, education, insurance, healthcare access, search visibility, moderation, identity resolution, or public reputation, the affected individual should have the right to notice, explanation, review, and challenge. Secret classifications and unreviewable algorithmic judgments have no legitimate place in a constitutional society that claims to value fairness, accountability, and equal protection in practice.

Large digital platforms must also be subject to procedural obligations when they exercise quasi-public power over speech, identity, access, and visibility. A platform capable of suppressing discoverability, limiting participation, flagging accounts, shaping search prominence, or contributing to reputational harm should not be free to act without standards. Notice, appeal, record access, and meaningful review should be baseline expectations wherever digital systems exercise substantial control over a person’s ability to participate in public or professional life.

Congress should further protect the public’s right to use strong encryption, secure communications, and personally controlled devices. Americans should not be required to weaken their own security in order to satisfy policy preferences that erode privacy for everyone. Secure technology is not a threat to freedom. It is one of the last practical defenses against abuse, intrusion, overcollection, identity exploitation, and unauthorized surveillance.

This issue is not abstract. Right now, individuals are expected to monitor breaches, chase down false records, remove themselves from predatory broker sites, interpret unreadable policies, navigate manipulative consent structures, and endure automated consequences they often cannot even inspect. Meanwhile, corporations and intermediaries continue to accumulate informational power on a scale no ordinary citizen can realistically match. That imbalance is not compatible with a free society.

The legislative branch has both the authority and the obligation to correct this. Congress should move immediately toward comprehensive federal digital rights legislation with enforceable standards, strong oversight, and real remedies. Any bill that preserves broad loopholes, weak preemption structures, nominal disclosure duties, or industry-friendly safe harbors while leaving the public exposed will fail the moment it is passed. The country does not need another diluted framework written around the comfort of powerful intermediaries. It needs law that restores power to the individual.

The central question is simple: whether the American people will remain sovereign over their own identities, records, communications, and digital existence, or whether those domains will continue to be governed by opaque systems and commercial actors with minimal accountability. Rights that collapse online are not meaningful rights. Freedom that exists only outside digital life is no longer sufficient for modern citizenship.

I urge the House and Senate to act with clarity, force, and seriousness. The public deserves federal protection worthy of the scale of the problem. Digital freedom is not optional, and delay is no longer defensible.

Respectfully,

**Justin-Ames Gamache, M.Ed., M.S.**  
Citizen, scholar, and advocate for digital freedom
